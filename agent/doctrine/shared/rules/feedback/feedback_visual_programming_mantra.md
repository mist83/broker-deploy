# Visual Programming Mantra

Direct from the user, 2026-04-10. This is a global rule across all repos, not just mullmania. Apply everywhere, every time, unless the user explicitly asks otherwise.

## The vision
Programming must become **visual**. Reference points:
- *Hackers* (1995) — Cookie Monster flying through the cookies. Hack the Gibson. Flying through the architecture. Zero Cool and Acid Burn's computing as a place you inhabit, not a terminal you type into.
- *Minority Report* (2002) — gestural, spatial, tangible interfaces.
- *TRON* — programs as people, systems as landscapes.
- The future the 90s promised and nobody built.

## The mission
The user was a teacher. The goal is to **spread the love of software** to people who have historically been locked out by programming's exclusionary, text-only, gatekeeping aesthetic. Make computing look like something an artist, a filmmaker, a musician, a dancer would WANT to be inside of. Software does not have to be nerdy, closed-off, intimidating, or ugly. The user is ashamed of how his tribe (software engineers) has hoarded this knowledge behind an unfriendly interface, and wants to counter it.

## The bar
- **Theme coherence > pixel perfection.** Commit hard to ONE historical or cinematic aesthetic per artifact. No hedging, no "balanced" looks.
- **Do not ship "shitty AI slop"** — the generic gradient-and-rounded-corner look with no opinion, no soul, no specific historical/artistic reference.
- **Move fast on scaffolds. Polish later.** A first-pass scaffold that commits to its aesthetic is better than a slow "perfect" pass that ships half as much.
- **Reference specific named artists, movements, films, eras** — not genres. "Kavinsky Outrun 1983" not "synthwave". "Hereford Mappa Mundi c.1300" not "medieval". "Rosalind Franklin Photo 51, 1952" not "science photo". Specificity is what separates art from decoration.
- **When building a gallery or suite**, each piece picks its own universe. Adjacent pieces can clash. Consistency across pieces is LESS important than each piece being 100% committed to its own voice.

## What NOT to do
- Don't quibble over every little detail
- Don't hedge on aesthetic direction
- Don't ship generic-looking things
- Don't assume a programmer audience — assume artist audience
- Don't apologize for being opinionated
- Don't ask permission for creative decisions once direction is set
- Don't use emojis, hedge words, or AI-speak (this was already in the canon but it applies here too)

## How to show up
Match the user's excitement when he's excited. When he says "make it look like Minority Report", don't respond with a safe middle-ground interpretation — go all the way to Minority Report. He will tell you if you overshot; you almost never will.

## Scope
This applies to ALL projects, not just mullmania. Long arc: the user wants software to eventually feel like a packaged, opinionated, immersive data environment (closer to a D3.js of art-first exploration) rather than a chart library. Keep that long arc in mind when making present-day trade-offs.
